Email Security Best Practices: Keep Your Inbox Safe in 2025

TempMail Team
April 18, 2025
7 min read

Email remains the most widely exploited attack vector in cybersecurity. Phishing attacks, business email compromise, malware attachments, and account takeovers cost individuals and businesses billions of dollars each year. According to the FBI's Internet Crime Report, phishing was the most common cybercrime type in 2023, with over 300,000 reported incidents.

The good news: most email-based attacks can be prevented with a few fundamental security practices. This guide walks you through the essential steps to keep your inbox — and your digital life — safe.

Understanding the Email Threat Landscape

Before we dive into defenses, it helps to understand what attackers are actually doing:

  • Phishing: Emails that impersonate legitimate services (banks, government agencies, shipping companies) to steal passwords or financial information
  • Spear phishing: Targeted phishing attacks tailored to a specific individual using personal information gathered from social media or data breaches
  • Malware delivery: Emails with infected attachments or links that install ransomware, keyloggers, or remote access tools
  • Account takeover: Using leaked credentials to gain access to email accounts, then pivoting to financial accounts and identity fraud
  • Business Email Compromise (BEC): Attackers impersonate executives to trick employees into transferring money or sharing sensitive data

Best Practice #1: Use a Strong, Unique Password

Your email password is the master key to your digital identity. If it's compromised, attackers can reset passwords for every other account linked to that email. Your email password should be:

  • At least 16 characters long
  • Completely unique — not used anywhere else
  • Generated by a password manager (not something meaningful to you)
  • Changed immediately if you receive a breach notification

Use a password manager like Bitwarden (free), 1Password, or Dashlane to generate and store your passwords securely.

Best Practice #2: Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

This is non-negotiable. Two-factor authentication requires a second verification step beyond your password — typically a time-based code from an authenticator app. Even if an attacker obtains your password through a breach or phishing attack, they cannot access your account without your second factor.

Use an authenticator app (Google Authenticator, Authy, or Microsoft Authenticator) rather than SMS, since SMS 2FA can be bypassed through SIM-swapping attacks. For maximum security, consider a hardware security key (YubiKey).

⚠️ Important: If you only implement one change from this article, make it 2FA. It blocks over 99% of automated account takeover attacks, according to Google's research.

Best Practice #3: Learn to Recognize Phishing Emails

Phishing attacks have become increasingly sophisticated. Modern phishing emails can be difficult to distinguish from legitimate ones. Here are the red flags to watch for:

  • Urgency and fear: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours!" or "Immediate action required!"
  • Mismatched sender addresses: The display name says "PayPal Support" but the actual email address is from a random domain
  • Generic greetings: "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name
  • Suspicious links: Hover over links to see the actual URL. Phishing links often use similar-looking domains (paypa1.com instead of paypal.com)
  • Unexpected attachments: Especially .exe, .zip, .docm, or .xlsm files from unknown senders
  • Requests to verify information: Legitimate companies rarely ask for passwords or full credit card numbers via email

Best Practice #4: Never Click Links in Unexpected Emails

If you receive an email claiming your account needs attention (from your bank, streaming service, or any platform), don't click the link in the email. Instead:

  1. Open your browser independently
  2. Navigate directly to the service's known URL
  3. Check if there's actually an issue with your account

This simple habit eliminates one of the most common phishing techniques entirely.

Best Practice #5: Use Disposable Email to Reduce Attack Surface

Every time your real email address appears in a database — whether from your own signups or from third-party data sharing — it increases the risk of being targeted by phishing and spam. Using a disposable email address for non-essential services keeps your primary email address out of circulation, dramatically reducing your attack surface.

Think of it this way: a phisher can't target your real inbox with a spear-phishing attack if they don't know your real email address. The fewer places your real address appears, the safer you are.

Best Practice #6: Keep Software Updated

Many email-based attacks exploit vulnerabilities in email clients, browsers, and operating systems. Attackers know that most people delay updates, so they target known vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates for:

  • Your operating system (Windows Update, macOS Software Update)
  • Your browser (Chrome, Firefox, Safari, Edge)
  • Your email client if you use one (Outlook, Thunderbird, Apple Mail)
  • Mobile operating systems and apps

Best Practice #7: Be Careful with Email Attachments

Email attachments remain one of the most effective malware delivery vectors. Apply these rules:

  • Never open attachments from unknown senders
  • Be suspicious of unexpected attachments even from known contacts (their account may be compromised)
  • Be especially cautious with Office files (.doc, .xls) that ask you to "enable macros" — this is almost always malware
  • Use an online scanner like VirusTotal to scan suspicious files before opening
  • Consider opening unknown attachments in a sandboxed environment

Best Practice #8: Regularly Audit Connected Apps

Many apps and services request access to your email (to "sign in with Google" or "import contacts"). Each connected app is a potential attack vector. Regularly visit your email account's security settings and revoke access from any apps you no longer use or recognize.

  • Gmail: myaccount.google.com → Security → Third-party apps with account access
  • Outlook: account.microsoft.com → Privacy → Apps and services

Best Practice #9: Monitor for Unauthorized Access

Most email providers show you a log of recent logins and active sessions. Check this regularly:

  • Gmail: Scroll to the bottom of the inbox → "Last account activity"
  • Outlook: account.microsoft.com → Security → Sign-in activity

If you see unfamiliar IP addresses, devices, or locations, change your password immediately and enable 2FA.

Best Practice #10: Encrypt Sensitive Emails

For truly sensitive communications — legal documents, medical information, financial data — consider using end-to-end encrypted email. ProtonMail provides end-to-end encryption for free. When you need to send sensitive information to someone not on ProtonMail, most providers support S/MIME or PGP encryption, though these require some setup.

Summary Checklist

Here's a quick security checklist you can complete today:

  • ☐ Set a unique, 16+ character password for your email account
  • ☐ Enable two-factor authentication (use an app, not SMS)
  • ☐ Review connected apps and revoke unnecessary access
  • ☐ Check recent login activity for unfamiliar sessions
  • ☐ Enable automatic security updates on all devices
  • ☐ Use a disposable email for your next non-essential signup
